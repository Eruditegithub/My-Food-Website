THE DISH THAT RAISED ME!


Amala, Ewedu, and My Mother's Hands

If home had a flavor, mine would taste like Amala. Ewedu soup, and tender meat - soft, rich, and deeply familiar. It wasn't just a meal. It was a tradition, a rhythm, a language spoken between my mother and me long before I understood the power of food.

Every Saturday morning, like clockwork, the smell would begin to creep through the house. The earthy scent of yam flour slowly whisked into a dark, steamy swirl - Amala, always smooth, never lumpy. Then came the green - the Ewedu leaves, beaten to a velvet consistency, laced with locust beans and a hint of magic I still can't replicate.

And finally, the meat. Oh, the meat. Soft, seasoned to the bone, simmered until it fell apart under the weight of my fork - or, more often, my eager fingers.

I'd hover nearby as my mum moved effortlessly between pots, no recipe in sight, just inict, memory, and love. She cooked like she was telling a story - of where we came from, of who we were. In the moments between her stirring and seasoning, I learned more than cooking: I learned care, pride, and patience.

Sitting down to eat, we didn't talk much. We didn't need to. Every swallow of Amala coated in green Ewedu, soaked in spicy stew, said enough. It said welcome. It said home.

To this day, no matter where I find myself, Amala and Ewedu is more than a dish - it's a piece of my mother's heart, a memory I can taste, and a reminder that "the food that raises you never really leaves you."
